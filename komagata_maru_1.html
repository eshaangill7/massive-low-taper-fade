About the Komagata Maru

The Voyage · 1914

The Komagata Maru was a ship that carried Indian travellers from India, specifically Punjab, to Vancouver BC, in Canada. This voyage took place in 1914. On that ship there were 376 passengers, 340 Sikhs, 24 Muslims, and 12 Hindus. This ship was led by Gurdit Singh. The passengers were denied entry into Canada and were forced back to India, where they were shot at when they returned.

Gurdit Singh

Gurdit Singh chartered the Komagata Maru deliberately to challenge discriminatory Canadian immigration laws. As a prosperous Sikh businessman, he sought to test whether non-white British subjects held the same rights of movement within the British Empire as their white counterparts.

"We are British subjects and we consider we have a perfect right to visit any part of the British Empire." — Gurdit Singh

What Happened in Canada

Vancouver, British Columbia · 1914

The Komagata Maru sits in Burrard Inlet for two months, refused entry · Public Domain

Upon arriving in Vancouver harbour, the passengers were met not with welcome, but with a wall of legal obstacles, racial hostility, and deliberate cruelty designed to force them to turn back. The Canadian government systematically denied the passengers their rights as British subjects.

The Vancouver Sun referred to the passengers as "Hindu invaders" — a reflection of the widespread racism driving government policy.

Significant Events

  • The Canadian government used the Continuous Journey Regulation to immediately deny entrance, since the ship took stops in Hong Kong and Japan.
  • The government strictly enforced the rule that immigrants must have tickets purchased in their country of birth, which was impossible for many passengers on the ship.
  • The authorities claimed the passengers did not have the required $200 in their possession. At the time, $200 was a massive sum of money.
  • "Medical inspections" were used to stall the ship's progress while refusing to let passengers off for proper assessments.
  • The ship sat in the Burrard Inlet at the Port of Vancouver for 2 months.
  • The passengers were denied contact with their lawyer, J. Edward Bird.
  • Immigration inspector Malcolm Reid personally blocked the passengers' lawyer from boarding the ship to provide legal advice.
  • Officials used a test case (Munshi Singh) to consolidate the legal challenge and ensure a swift, singular rejection.
  • The local South Asian community formed a Shore Committee to provide legal aid and financial support for the passengers' challenge.
  • The government blocked the Shore Committee from bringing food and water to the ship.
  • The passengers often had to go up to 48 hours without food or water.
  • Only approximately 20 passengers could prove prior Canadian residency and were allowed to enter Canada.
  • Locals used racism as a motivating factor, wanting to keep a "White Canada."
  • The Canadian press often demonized the passengers, with publications like the Vancouver Sun calling them "Hindu invaders."
  • The voyage was a deliberate challenge led by Gurdit Singh to test whether non-white British subjects had the same rights as white ones.
  • The Canadian government refused to recognize the passengers' status as British subjects with the right to move within the Empire.
  • They tried to force the ship to turn back by restricting basic necessities.
  • When the passengers resisted, the government deployed the HMCS Rainbow — Canada's first ever permanent warship — to intimidate them into leaving.
  • The crew of the HMCS Rainbow pointed guns at the ship and made threatening gestures toward the passengers.

What Happened in India

Budge Budge, near Calcutta · September 1914

After being expelled from Canada, the passengers returned not to safety, but to an even more violent reception. By the time the ship arrived back in India, the political climate had shifted dramatically — World War I had begun, and the British colonial government viewed the returning passengers with deep suspicion and hostility.

The returning passengers were labelled "Ghadaries" — revolutionaries who had adopted western ideas of equality — and were treated as enemies of the Crown.

Passengers arriving at Budge Budge, near Calcutta, September 1914 · Public Domain

Significant Events

  • Before the ship even arrived back in India, the British government had passed the Ingress into India Ordinance — a law allowing the government to restrict the liberty of anyone entering India.
  • By the time the ship returned to India in September 1914, World War I had begun, dramatically heightening colonial suspicion.
  • The returning passengers were seen as "Ghadaries" — meaning revolutionaries — accused of adopting western ideas of equality, even though colonial forces in both Canada and India were working together against them.
  • They returned to a port in a town called Budge Budge, near Calcutta.
  • Passengers were met by a large detachment of British soldiers and police rather than immigration officers.
  • Passengers were ordered to board a train that would take them directly to Punjab without stopping.
  • When a group of passengers attempted to walk toward Calcutta to visit a Gurdwara (Sikh temple), police opened fire on the crowd.
  • After passengers refused to board the train, British forces opened fire, killing 20 people.
  • More than 200 passengers were detained without charge.
  • Many passengers fled to the countryside but were eventually captured.
  • Passengers who were not imprisoned had permanent surveillance placed on their homes by the British.
  • The ship's leader, Gurdit Singh, was forced into hiding for 7 years to avoid execution or life imprisonment.

The Ghadar Party

Founded San Francisco, November 1913

Founded in San Francisco in November 1913, the Ghadar Party was a secular, revolutionary organization of Indian immigrants committed to overthrowing British colonial rule in India. The word "Ghadar" means revolt or mutiny in Urdu and Punjabi — a name that left no ambiguity about the organization's intentions.

The party was primarily made up of Punjabi Sikh immigrants living in North America who had experienced first-hand the racial discrimination and legal exclusion imposed upon them despite their status as British subjects. This injustice transformed many from labourers and merchants into committed anti-colonial revolutionaries.

The Ghadar Party published a newspaper — also called Ghadar — in multiple languages including Urdu, Punjabi, Hindi, and Gujarati, distributed widely across Indian diaspora communities around the world. The paper spread revolutionary ideas and called on Indians to rise up against British rule.

The experiences of passengers on the Komagata Maru were deeply connected to the Ghadar movement. Many passengers were suspected of Ghadar sympathies, which is precisely why the British colonial government in India treated the returning passengers as dangerous revolutionaries rather than ordinary travellers.

"Ghadar" — the word means revolt. The party's very name was a declaration of war against colonial oppression.

The Ghadar Party's legacy endured long after its founding. Its ideals of secular Indian nationalism, racial equality, and anti-imperialism influenced the broader Indian independence movement, and its members are remembered today as early freedom fighters who challenged empire from across the ocean.

Key Facts

  • Founded: November 1913, San Francisco, USA
  • Founders: Lala Har Dayal & Sohan Singh Bhakna
  • Type: Secular, revolutionary, anti-colonial
  • Members: Primarily Punjabi Sikh immigrants in North America
  • Publication: The Ghadar newspaper in multiple languages
  • Goal: Overthrow British colonial rule in India through armed revolt
  • Komagata Maru Link: Returning passengers were labelled "Ghadaries" by British authorities
